Retirement Savings Gaps May Increase Burden on Pennsylvania Taxpayers

Data shows insufficient savings among workers in all 67 counties

Overview

Workers in the United States accumulate the vast majority of their retirement savings through employer-based plans, but large gaps in coverage exist. Pennsylvania is no exception, with about 1 in 3 workers lacking access to a workplace plan.

As Pennsylvania workers age, inadequate retirement savings not only will force a reduction in the quality of life for many of them entering their golden years but also will put pressure on public spending and increase taxpayer burdens. According to The Pew Charitable Trusts in consultation with Econsult Solutions Inc., from a 2015 baseline through 2030, Pennsylvania can expect to see an additional $14.3 billion in state spending due to insufficient savings. This obligation will be borne by an ever-smaller share of the overall population. The dependency ratio—the ratio of working-age households to those aged 65 and over—is expected to grow by nearly 50%, further stretching the tax base. In 2015, for every 100 households aged 20-64, there were 38 households aged 65 and over. In 2030, that number will grow to 57 households aged 65 and over for every 100 working-age households.

Fortunately, even small savings now could offset this savings shortfall. The study finds that if households saved just $1,170 a year—or about $98 a month—over this period, they could completely erase the retirement savings gap and allow for a retirement without lifestyle changes driven by inadequate savings.

In 2030, on average, residents aged 65 and older in Adams County with a household income of less than $75,000 can expect an annual $3,980 income shortfall due to insufficient savings. Without new saving, county taxpayers will need to pay a cumulative $3,472 from 2015 to 2030 to maintain existing social services to older residents. The burden is exacerbated by the fact that the aged dependency ratio—the number of aged 65 and older households to households aged 20-64—will rise by 54%.

However, household savings of just $960 a year—$80 a month—over this period can erase the gap and allow for a retirement without lifestyle changes driven by inadequate savings.
